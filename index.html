Understanding Cliques in Graph Theory

Definition: A clique in a graph G is a subset of vertices where every two vertices are connected by an edge, forming a complete subgraph.

Complete Subgraph: A clique is essentially a complete subgraph where all possible edges between vertices exist.

Examples:

  • A single vertex is a clique of size 1.
  • An edge between two vertices forms a clique of size 2.
  • A triangle (three vertices all connected) is a clique of size 3.

Maximal Clique: A clique that cannot be extended by adding any vertex from the graph without breaking the complete connection is called a maximal clique. It is not necessarily the largest clique in size but is the largest within its neighborhood.

Formal Definition: A subgraph H of a graph G is a maximal clique if H is isomorphic to a complete graph and there exists no vertex v in V(G)\V(H) such that v is connected to all vertices of H.

Image Description:
The red subgraph in the first image is not a clique (missing an edge). The second is a non-maximal clique as an external vertex connects to all its members. The third and fourth are both maximal cliques, as no external vertex connects to all their vertices, even though they differ in size.

Clique Graph: A graph where each vertex represents a maximal clique of the original graph, and two vertices are connected if their corresponding cliques share at least one vertex.

Applications: Cliques are heavily used in:

  • Social network analysis (detecting tightly connected groups).
  • Bioinformatics (protein interaction networks).
  • Computer vision (object recognition).
  • Database design and cybersecurity.

Computational Complexity: Finding the largest clique (maximum clique problem) is NP-complete — a central challenge in algorithm design and complexity theory.
